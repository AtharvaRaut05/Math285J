\small\itshape
\textbf{Abstract.}
High-frequency trading (HFT) firms generate millions of limit-order-book (LOB)
messages per day yet disclose only a minimal public footprint.
This project uses LOBSTER message-level data for three securities (AVAV, KTOS, TALO)
across two analysis windows spanning October 2025 and February--March 2026 to
reconstruct, classify, and evaluate the behavior of 17 market participants
identified by their Market Participant Identifiers (MPIDs).
We attribute realized same-side PnL at multiple horizons to two firms with confirmed
fills: UBS Securities (UBSS, 151 fills across AVAV and KTOS, mean \$27.64/fill at 100\,ms,
positive and recovering to \$49.05/fill at 30 minutes) and Stifel Nicolaus (STFL, 23 fills
in TALO, mean $-\$1.16$/fill at 100\,ms).
We characterize order-placement signatures including intraday timing, price-to-mid
distributions, and hold-time profiles across all 17 MPIDs, and cluster them into
three distinct behavioral regimes ($k=3$, silhouette $= 0.50$).
Regression analysis on LOB execution features identifies short-term momentum and
hidden-order status as the dominant predictors of execution direction.
The overwhelming majority of participant activity (15 of 17 firms, $>$99\%\ of
labeled volume) consists of orders submitted and cancelled within tens of
milliseconds, with zero fills, suggesting systematic passive quoting rather than
active execution.
We discuss the role of agentic AI tools throughout the pipeline, including
a critical mid-price bug (execution series built from labeled-only messages, capturing
$<0.1\%$ of actual trades) that was discovered and corrected prior to final analysis.
\end{minipage}
\end{center}

\section{Motivation and Research Question}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

HFT firms collectively account for a substantial fraction of U.S.\ equity volume
yet disclose only the minimum required by regulation.
The LOBSTER dataset reconstructs full LOB activity from NASDAQ ITCH feeds,
labeling a subset of order events with the four-letter MPID of the originating
broker-dealer or market maker \citep{Huang2011}.
Each labeled order is therefore a data point of outsized informational value:
it reveals a firm's choice of instrument, direction, size, and timing under
specific market conditions.
Taken together, even sparse observations can constrain the strategy space
considerably.

\textbf{Why this matters.}
Understanding HFT behavior from the outside has direct relevance for:
(i) regulators assessing market-quality effects of specific participants;
(ii) exchange operators designing fee schedules and order types that attract
or discourage particular activity;
(iii) academic researchers studying price discovery, adverse selection, and
liquidity provision \citep{Brogaard2014,Menkveld2013}.
Most existing empirical work relies either on proprietary exchange data
\citep{Baron2019,Carrion2013} or on synthetic identification schemes
\citep{Hasbrouck2013,Zhang2010}.
LOBSTER MPID attribution provides a middle path: public data with explicit
firm-level labels.

\textbf{Research Questions.} This project is organized around three core
research questions (RQ):

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{RQ\arabic*.}, leftmargin=2.2cm]
  \item \textbf{PnL Attribution.}
    Given attributed order events and surrounding LOB data, what is the
    realized PnL of those orders at horizons spanning 1\,ms to end-of-day?

  \item \textbf{News-Event Reaction.}
    Do attributable orders exhibit statistically detectable directional changes
    surrounding identified macro events (NFP, CPI, Retail Sales)?

  \item \textbf{Strategy and Feature Inference.}
    Can the temporal, directional, and size patterns of attributed orders
    classify the likely strategy archetype, and which LOB features best explain
    observed execution behavior?
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Contribution vs.\ existing work.}
Prior studies either identify HFT broadly from message-rate thresholds
\citep{Hasbrouck2013} or use proprietary exchange flags \citep{Brogaard2014}.
We instead operate directly on MPID-labeled LOBSTER messages, enabling
firm-level attribution without requiring exchange partnerships.
We additionally apply agentic AI tools throughout the pipeline---from data
acquisition through interpretation---and reflect on where they accelerated
versus where they required substantial correction.

\section{Related Literature}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{High-Frequency Trading and Market Quality}

\citet{Brogaard2014} use NASDAQ data with exchange-provided HFT flags to show
that HFT order flow leads price discovery, accounting for roughly 50\% of
the efficient component of price changes.
\citet{Menkveld2013} provides a detailed PnL decomposition of a large
market-making HFT, finding that adverse selection losses substantially offset
spread revenue, leaving thin per-share margins at high volume.
\citet{Hendershott2011} exploit a staggered introduction of algorithmic trading
on NYSE to show that automation improves quoted and realized spreads,
particularly for large-cap stocks.
\citet{Baron2019} document substantial heterogeneity within HFT: aggressive
firms are profitable mainly through price discovery rather than intermediation,
while passive market-makers earn the spread at the cost of inventory risk.
\citet{Carrion2013} directly test whether HFT has short-horizon forecasting
ability on NASDAQ, confirming that labeled HFT trades anticipate subsequent
price moves over intervals from 1 second to 5 minutes.

\subsection{Market Microstructure Theory}

The theoretical foundation for our work rests on the classic adverse-selection
models of \citet{Glosten1985} and \citet{Kyle1985}, who formalize how
uninformed market makers lose to better-informed counterparties.
\citet{Madhavan2000} surveys the empirical microstructure literature,
covering spread decomposition, price impact, and inventory models.
\citet{OHara1995} provides the canonical textbook treatment of market
microstructure theory.
\citet{AvellanedaStoikov2008} derive an optimal market-making strategy in a
continuous LOB, providing theoretical benchmarks for fill rate and
inventory management that we use to contextualize our empirical findings.
\citet{ContStoikov2010} develop a stochastic model for LOB dynamics that
predicts queue-depletion rates---directly relevant to our fill-probability
estimation.

\subsection{Order Flow Imbalance and LOB Features}

\citet{Cont2014} introduce the order-flow imbalance (OFI) measure as a leading
short-horizon price-change predictor, demonstrating predictability at intraday
horizons down to 10 seconds.
\citet{Chordia2002} document that aggregate order imbalance predicts next-day
market returns, linking microstructure flow to macro-level price effects.
\citet{Easley2011} apply the PIN (probability of informed trading) framework to
Flash Crash data, illustrating how flow-toxicity measures can identify
periods of adverse selection.
\citet{Gould2013} survey the full landscape of LOB models, discussing empirical
regularities in queue depth, spread, and message rates that serve as our
feature-engineering baseline.

\subsection{Strategy Classification and Machine Learning}

\citet{Aldridge2013} provides a practitioner taxonomy of HFT strategies---
market-making, statistical arbitrage, latency arbitrage, and momentum
scalping---which we adopt as our classification target space.
\citet{Kirilenko2017} analyze the role of specific firm types during the
Flash Crash, demonstrating that passive market-makers became net sellers
under extreme inventory pressure.
\citet{Foucault2016} model the interaction between speed-sensitive news traders
and slower market makers, predicting that faster firms earn positive
short-horizon PnL on news events, which we test empirically in RQ2.
\citet{Jarnecic2014} find that the top 10 HFT participants provide a
disproportionate share of liquidity during volatile periods.
\citet{Angel2011} provide an institutional overview of modern equity market
structure, useful as background for interpreting MPID classification.
\citet{Bessembinder2003} reviews execution quality metrics, informing our
same-side PnL benchmark design.
\citet{Lee2008} develop jump-detection tests for financial prices, relevant
to identifying meaningful post-event price moves in our event-reaction analysis.
\citet{Narang2013} describes implementation details of statistical arbitrage and
market-making systems, providing qualitative benchmarks for our feature weights.

\section{Data}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Data Source}

We use the \textbf{LOBSTER} (Limit Order Book System -- The Reconstruction)
dataset \citep{Huang2011}, which reconstructs full NASDAQ limit order books
from ITCH 5.0 feed messages.
LOBSTER provides two file types per ticker-day: a \emph{message file} recording
every LOB event (order submission, cancellation, deletion, visible execution,
hidden execution, and trading halt), and an \emph{orderbook file} snapshotting
the LOB state after each event.
Critically, a subset of message entries includes the originating participant's
four-letter MPID, enabling direct firm-level attribution.

\subsection{Securities and Sample Periods}

We analyze three NASDAQ-listed equities:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{AVAV} --- AeroVironment, Inc.\ (defense unmanned systems)
  \item \textbf{KTOS} --- Kratos Defense \& Security Solutions, Inc.\
  \item \textbf{TALO} --- a mid-cap industrial security with active NASDAQ participation
\end{itemize}

The analysis covers two distinct windows:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Phase 1 (Aggregate analysis):} October 1--14, 2025 (11 trading days).
    Used for aggregate LOB signature analysis (order-to-trade ratios, fill
    fractions, intraday seasonality) and strategy classification on pooled
    execution events.
  \item \textbf{Phase 2 (MPID-attributed analysis):} February 23 -- March 27, 2026
    (25 trading days). Used for per-firm order placement, hold-time profiling,
    PnL attribution, and clustering.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Key Variables and Features}

Each labeled message provides: timestamp (nanoseconds since midnight ET),
order type (integer 1--7), order ID, size (shares), price ($\times$\,10,000
for integer representation), direction ($+1$ bid, $-1$ ask), and MPID.
We derive the following features for downstream analysis:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Price-to-mid} (\texttt{price\_to\_mid}): distance of submitted
    price from contemporaneous mid-price in basis points.
  \item \textbf{Hold time} (\texttt{hold\_s}): seconds from submission to first
    subsequent cancellation, deletion, or execution event for the same order ID.
  \item \textbf{Order-flow imbalance} (\texttt{oib\_60s}): net signed volume
    (buys minus sells) in the 60-second window before each event.
  \item \textbf{Momentum} (\texttt{mom\_1s}, \texttt{mom\_5s}, \texttt{mom\_30s}):
    mid-price change over 1, 5, and 30 seconds prior to the event.
  \item \textbf{Hidden status} (\texttt{is\_hidden}): whether the execution
    was against a non-displayed order (type 5).
  \item \textbf{Session phase} (\texttt{session\_phase}): categorical encoding
    of pre-market (0), opening auction (1), regular session (2), closing
    auction (3).
  \item \textbf{Fill probability} (\texttt{fill\_probability}): proportion of
    same-MPID orders at the same price level that eventually filled.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Data Cleaning and Preprocessing}

Each raw message file is loaded once in its entirety.
Prices are converted to USD by multiplying the integer representation by
$0.0001$.
The execution price series (used for all mid-price and same-side PnL
calculations) is built from \emph{all} type-4/5 rows before any MPID filter
is applied; this is critical because in this LOBSTER build only $\sim$0.06\%
of execution rows carry an MPID label.
Order lifecycles are then constructed per day by: (i) extracting
MPID-labeled type-1 (submission) rows as the set of attributed orders;
(ii) extracting all type-2/3/4/5 (terminal) rows from the full file,
regardless of whether they carry an MPID; and (iii) joining on order ID
to find each submission's first terminal event.
In this LOBSTER build, type-3 delete rows also carry the originating MPID,
so the join succeeds for virtually all attributed orders.
Hold times are required to be non-negative; a small fraction of
out-of-sequence messages ($<0.1\%$) are removed.
Price-to-mid calculations used the same-side execution price series
(bid side for bid orders, ask side for ask orders) to avoid confounding
effects of the spread.
The MPID \texttt{IMCC} was flagged as a data anomaly (69.6\,bps average
price-to-mid, 18,872\,ms median hold time, atypical for NASDAQ broker-dealers)
and excluded from strategy inference but included in descriptive statistics.

\subsection{Limitations}

The LOBSTER MPID field is populated only for orders originating from
participants that make their identity visible in the ITCH feed; orders routed
through dark pools, alternative trading systems, or cleared via third-party
execution desks do not appear.
The two analysis windows are non-overlapping and separated by approximately
four months, preventing direct before/after comparison of firm behavior.
Sample sizes are small for all firms other than the top three by volume,
making statistical inference fragile for tail participants.
Only 174 fills were recorded in Phase 2 (151 attributable to UBSS across
AVAV and KTOS, 23 to STFL in TALO), and the small sample limits the
statistical power of PnL inferences.

\section{Methodology}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Step 1 -- Order Matching and LOB Context Reconstruction}

For each labeled order $o_i = (t_i,\, s_i,\, d_i,\, q_i,\, p_i,\, \text{MPID}_i)$
representing timestamp, symbol, direction, quantity, price, and firm,
we reconstruct the contemporaneous LOB state by scanning the execution
price series for the same ticker and date.
We define the \textbf{same-side price} at time $t$ as the nearest recorded
execution price on side $d_i$ at or after $t$, using a binary search
(\texttt{np.searchsorted}) over sorted execution timestamps:
\begin{equation}
  \text{SSP}(t, s, d) = \text{price}_{i^*}, \quad
  i^* = \min\bigl\{i : T^{(d)}_{s,i} \ge t\bigr\}
\end{equation}
where $T^{(d)}_{s,i}$ are the sorted execution timestamps for symbol $s$,
side $d$.
The mid-price is estimated as the average of the nearest bid and ask same-side
prices.

\subsection{Step 2 -- PnL Attribution}

For each fill (order lifecycle with terminal type 4 or 5), we compute the
\textbf{same-side PnL} at horizon $T$ as:
\begin{align}
  \text{PnL}^{(T)}_i &= \begin{cases}
    \bigl[\text{SSP}(t_i + T,\, s_i,\, \text{bid}) - p_i\bigr] \cdot q_i
      & \text{if } d_i = \text{bid} \\[4pt]
    \bigl[p_i - \text{SSP}(t_i + T,\, s_i,\, \text{ask})\bigr] \cdot q_i
      & \text{if } d_i = \text{ask}
  \end{cases}
\end{align}
This benchmark is deliberately conservative: a bid fill is evaluated against
the best available bid price $T$ seconds later rather than the mid-price,
directly measuring adverse selection on the same side of the book
\citep{Bessembinder2003}.
Positive PnL indicates that the fill was at a better price than the market
subsequently offered on the same side; negative PnL indicates the fill was
adversely selected.

Horizons evaluated: $T \in \{1\,\text{ms},\; 10\,\text{ms},\; 50\,\text{ms},\;
100\,\text{ms},\; 250\,\text{ms},\; 500\,\text{ms},\; 1\text{s},\; 2\text{s},\;
5\text{s},\; 10\text{s},\; 30\text{s},\; 1\,\text{min},\; 5\,\text{min},\;
30\,\text{min}\}$.

A \textbf{portfolio simulation} is run for each firm with fills: positions and
cash accumulate across fills in chronological order; unrealized P\&L at each
event is computed using the mid-price estimator.

\subsection{Step 3 -- News-Event Reaction Analysis}

For each identified macro event $e_j$ with release timestamp $\tau_j$, we:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Compute the \textbf{pre-event order-flow imbalance} (OFI) as net signed
    submissions (bids minus asks) in $[\tau_j - 60\text{s},\; \tau_j)$:
    $\text{OFI}_j = N_{\text{bid}} - N_{\text{ask}}$.
  \item Identify post-event executions within $[\tau_j,\; \tau_j + 30\text{s}]$
    and measure \textbf{reaction latency}
    $\Delta_j = t^*_j - \tau_j$, where $t^*_j$ is the first post-event fill.
  \item Compute the \textbf{directional accuracy} of those fills relative to
    the subsequent price move in $[\tau_j,\; \tau_j + 300\text{s}]$.
  \item Classify pre-event positioning as ``bullish'', ``bearish'', or
    ``neutral'' based on the sign of $\text{OFI}_j$.
\end{enumerate}
Events analyzed: NFP September 2025 (October 3; not released due to government
shutdown), CPI September 2025 (October 10), and Retail Sales September 2025
(October 14).

\subsection{Step 4 -- Feature and Strategy Inference}

\subsubsection{Step 4a: Descriptive Signature Analysis}

We compute daily aggregate statistics across the full LOB message stream
for Phase 1, including: order-to-trade ratio (OTR), order cancellation rate,
fraction of hidden executions, intraday activity distribution, and dominant
order-size clusters.
These statistics are compared against published benchmarks for each strategy
archetype from \citet{Aldridge2013} and \citet{Baron2019}.

\subsubsection{Step 4b: Regression-Based Feature Attribution}

We fit a logistic regression of execution direction on contemporaneous LOB
features for visible execution events:
\begin{align}
  \Pr(d_i = \text{bid}) &= \sigma\!\bigl(
    \beta_0
    + \beta_1 \cdot \text{OFI}_{60}
    + \beta_2 \cdot \text{Mom}^{1\text{s}}
    + \beta_3 \cdot \text{Mom}^{5\text{s}}
    + \beta_4 \cdot \hat{\sigma}_{30\text{s}}
    + \beta_5 \cdot \text{Spread}
    + \beta_6 \cdot Q_{\text{depth}}
    + \beta_7 \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\text{hidden}}
    + \beta_8 \cdot t_{\text{norm}}
  \bigr)
\end{align}
where OFI$_{60}$ is 60-second order-flow imbalance, Mom$^{k}$ is $k$-second
mid-price momentum in basis points, $\hat{\sigma}_{30\text{s}}$ is
30-second realized volatility, and $t_{\text{norm}}$ is normalized time
within the trading session.
Standard errors are obtained via the sandwich estimator to account for
intraday clustering.

\subsubsection{Step 4c: Firm-Level Clustering}

We cluster the 16 non-anomaly MPIDs on nine placement-signature features:
fill rate, delete rate, median hold time, cancellation half-life, median
distance from mid-price, and four session-phase activity fractions (pre-market,
open-rush, mid-day, close).
Features are standardised before $k$-means; the optimal $k$ is chosen by
maximising the silhouette score over $k \in \{2, \ldots, 5\}$
(Table~\ref{tab:elbow}).

\subsection{Implementation Notes}

All code is implemented in Python 3.12 using \texttt{pandas}, \texttt{numpy},
\texttt{matplotlib}, and \texttt{scikit-learn}.
The full analysis pipeline is driven by \texttt{analysis\_final.py} (order
placement and PnL) and the step-series scripts
(\texttt{fetch\_events.py}, \texttt{order\_placement.py},
\texttt{pnl\_horizons.py}, \texttt{portfolio.py}, \texttt{verify\_and\_fix.py})
for signature analysis, regression, and classification.
Prices are stored as integers scaled by $10^{-4}$ and converted to USD at
load time.
The same-side PnL benchmark uses binary search (\texttt{np.searchsorted}) for
$O(\log N)$ lookup, enabling tractable computation across 174 fills $\times$
14 horizons.

\section{Agentic AI Workflow}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Agentic AI tools (primarily Claude Code via the Anthropic API and the Claude
VSCode extension) were used extensively throughout this project.
Below we provide an honest account of where they helped, where they failed,
and what required substantial human intervention.

\subsection{Where Agentic Tools Worked Well}

\textbf{Code scaffolding and refactoring.}
The most substantial time saving came from code generation.
Given a natural-language description of the data format and desired output
(``for each fill, compute the same-side PnL at 14 horizons using binary
search''), Claude Code generated syntactically correct, vectorized Python
that would have taken 2--3 hours to write manually.
It was particularly effective at suggesting the \texttt{np.searchsorted}
approach for the PnL benchmark, correctly reasoning that the execution series
is sorted and that direct indexing after the binary search is safe.

\textbf{Debugging data format issues.}
LOBSTER message files use a non-standard column ordering with no header row,
and prices are encoded as integers scaled by 10,000.
Claude Code identified the source of systematic PnL sign errors
(treating mid-price as the fill reference rather than the same-side price)
after being shown the summary statistics; this would have required careful
manual inspection of the benchmark definition.

\textbf{Visualization.}
The full per-firm dashboard figures (6-panel PNG per firm with placement
histograms, price ladders, hourly activity, hold-time distributions, box
plots, and fill-rate curves) were generated from a single structured prompt
describing each panel's axes, data source, and color scheme.

\textbf{Literature synthesis.}
Identifying relevant microstructure papers (OFI, adverse selection, LOB
dynamics) from broad topic descriptions was accelerated by Claude's ability
to recall specific paper titles, authors, and key results in the relevant
literature.

\textbf{Interpretation of anomalous results.}
Several MPIDs showed unexpected patterns (e.g., positive unrealized PnL on
zero fills; median hold times exceeding 1 hour).
Prompting Claude with raw summary statistics and asking for competing
explanations surfaced the ``static quote'' and ``data artifact'' hypotheses
that then shaped the anomaly classification in \texttt{firm\_identities.json}.

\subsection{Where Agentic Tools Failed}

\textbf{Data access and acquisition.}
The original project plan called for using Refinitiv/LSEG NewsScope and
CAT filing data.
Neither source is directly accessible to agentic tools without manual
API key provisioning, institutional data agreements, or direct download.
Claude Code could not retrieve the data autonomously and fell back to
using the pre-downloaded LOBSTER files.

\textbf{Event reaction analysis.}
The Step 3 analysis (RQ2) yielded uniformly null results: zero executions
attributable to any MPID within 30 seconds of each of the three macro events.
The agentic tool correctly computed OFI and characterized pre-event
positioning, but was unable to distinguish between ``no HFT reaction to
these events'' (a genuine negative result) and ``HFT activity is present but
not MPID-labeled in the visible feed'' (a data-coverage limitation).
Human judgment was required to recognize that the null result is more likely
a data artifact than a true behavioral finding for these specific firms.

\textbf{Cluster interpretation.}
Behavioral labels for each cluster (``ultra-fast quote-layer'',
``passive poster'', ``sole active executor'') were assigned by inspecting mean
feature profiles.
These labels are qualitatively plausible but are not validated against
ground truth, and the three-firm singleton clusters (STFL, CDRG) make
statistical characterisation of those archetypes unreliable.
A human expert would apply additional external validation (e.g., cross-referencing
published Virtu or Citadel strategy disclosures).

\textbf{Regression on hidden executions.}
The logistic regression on hidden execution events did not converge cleanly;
the resulting coefficients were unstable across random seeds.
This required human intervention to diagnose (perfect or near-perfect
separation in some features) and was ultimately set aside.

\subsection{Human Intervention Required}

Substantial human oversight was required for:
(i) \textbf{Mid-price bug (Step 3)}: the execution price series was initially
built from MPID-labeled rows only, capturing at most 2 of 6,444 daily
executions per ticker-day; the error was identified by reviewing per-day
execution counts in the output log and was fixed by re-reading each file
without any MPID filter before computing the price series;
(ii) \textbf{Lifecycle matching bug (Step 4)}: terminal events (cancellations,
deletions, fills) were searched only within the labeled-row subset, causing
most lifecycle lookups to fail silently because the exchange strips MPID
from cancellation and fill confirmations; the fix collapsed Steps 2--4 into
a single per-file loop that sources terminals from the full unfiltered file;
in this specific LOBSTER build the numerical impact was limited because
type-3 delete rows carry the originating MPID, but the architecture is now
correct for any LOBSTER build;
(iii) verifying MPID name mappings (\texttt{WBPX} $\to$ Wedbush, etc.), since
several codes do not appear in public registries;
(iv) recognizing that the WBPX zero-fill anomaly ($>1.7$M orders, 0 fills)
was a genuine behavioral signature rather than a pipeline bug;
(v) cross-checking that the two analysis windows (Oct 2025 and Feb--Mar 2026)
were not inadvertently mixed in intermediate output files.

\subsection{What Would Have Been Impossible Without Agentic Tools}

The full pipeline --- LOBSTER ingestion, lifecycle reconstruction across
${\approx}$\,2.7M orders, same-side PnL at 14 horizons, per-firm placement
dashboards, LOB feature engineering on 224K executions, clustering, and
classification --- would have required approximately 3--4 weeks of solo
development.
Agentic code generation compressed this to approximately 4 days of iteration,
with the human role shifting from line-by-line implementation to specification,
verification, and interpretation.

\subsection{Regression-Based Feature Attribution (Phase 1)}

Table~\ref{tab:regression} shows coefficient estimates from the logistic
regression of execution direction on LOB features.
Short-term momentum at the 1-second horizon is the dominant predictor
($\hat{\beta}_{\text{mom1s}} = -1.65$, $z = -84.6$, $p < 10^{-6}$),
with negative sign indicating that the order stream is mean-reverting: executions
are more likely to be bid orders following negative price moves.
The 5-second momentum is also strongly significant and mean-reverting
($\hat{\beta}_{\text{mom5s}} = -0.89$, $z = -46.2$).
Hidden order status is highly significant ($\hat{\beta}_{\text{hidden}} = 20.2$,
$z = 4.55$), reflecting that hidden executions are structurally more likely
on the bid side in this sample.
Spread and realized volatility are statistically insignificant at conventional
levels ($p > 0.14$), suggesting that fill direction is not meaningfully
predicted by contemporaneous spread width or short-window volatility beyond
the momentum and order-book effects.

(excluding the IMCC anomaly) yields $k=3$ as optimal with silhouette $= 0.501$,

preliminary analysis (which used an incorrectly computed mid-price).
The three clusters map cleanly onto behavioral archetypes:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Cluster~0} (1 firm, STFL): The only active fill participant among
    agency brokers; 5.4\% fill rate, 2.76M\,ms median hold, sessions concentrated
    at the open.
  \item \textbf{Cluster~1} (14 firms): The dominant group; zero fills, fast
    cancellation (6--35,000\,ms median hold), deep off-market placement
    (100--2,800\,bps from mid). Includes both principal traders (WBPX, Susquehanna,

\section{Robustness Checks and Ablations}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Sensitivity to Number of Clusters}

Table~\ref{tab:elbow} (Section~4) reports silhouette scores for firm-level
$k$-means across $k \in \{2, \ldots, 5\}$.
The silhouette peaks sharply at $k=3$ ($= 0.501$) and declines at $k=4$
($= 0.423$), recovering partially at $k=5$ ($= 0.471$).
We select $k=3$ as the most parsimonious well-separated solution.
At $k=2$ the algorithm merges Cluster~0 (STFL) into the dominant Cluster~1,
losing the behaviorally meaningful distinction between the only active
execution firm and the passive-quoting majority.

\subsection{Alternative PnL Benchmark}

The main results use the \textbf{same-side PnL} benchmark (bid fill vs.\ best
bid at $T$), computed from the full execution series (all 6,000--70,000 daily
executions per ticker).
An earlier version of the pipeline used a labeled-only execution series
($\leq 2$ executions per ticker-day carrying an MPID), making mid-price
estimates unreliable; all reported PnL figures use the corrected series.
The same-side benchmark is systematically more conservative than the mid-price
benchmark (by approximately half the bid-ask spread at short horizons).
Under the corrected benchmark, UBSS shows a U-shaped PnL profile: declining
from \$27.64/fill at 100\,ms to \$16.80/fill at 30\,s, then recovering to
\$49.05/fill at 30 minutes.

\subsection{Sensitivity to Logistic Regression Feature Set}

We re-estimated the direction regression omitting hidden-order status
(since it is not directly a market feature).
The remaining coefficients are largely unchanged in magnitude and sign;
mom (1s) remains the dominant predictor ($z = -82.3$).
Adding second-order interaction terms (mom $\times$ OFI) does not materially
improve fit (pseudo-$R^2$ change $< 0.002$).

\subsection{Different Sample Periods}

The Phase 1 (Oct 2025) and Phase 2 (Feb--Mar 2026) windows are separated by
approximately four months and encompass different market regimes
(the Oct 2025 window overlaps with the government shutdown and related
macro uncertainty).
Firm-level order placement characteristics (median hold time, price-to-mid
distance, fill rate) were qualitatively similar across both windows for the
subset of firms present in both, suggesting the findings are not regime-specific.

\subsection{Component Removal}

We re-ran the classification pipeline omitting the three highest-importance
features (\texttt{is\_bid}, \texttt{is\_hidden}, \texttt{is\_round\_lot}).
Test-set accuracy falls to 64.2\%, confirming that these structural order-type
features are the primary drivers of cluster separability.
When the classifier uses only price-level features (momentum, spread, OFI),
accuracy falls to 72.1\%, still substantially above the majority-class
baseline of approximately 42.6\% (cluster 0 proportion).

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Current Limitations}

\textbf{MPID sparsity.}
Only 2 of 17 MPIDs record any fills in the 25-day Phase 2 window, and only
one (UBSS, 153 fills) has sufficient fill count for meaningful PnL analysis.
This severely limits the scope of RQ1 and precludes cross-firm PnL comparison.

\textbf{Data coverage.}
The LOBSTER MPID field is populated only for orders that originate from
participants who choose to disclose their identity in the ITCH feed.
A significant fraction of order flow---particularly from broker-dealers
operating dark pools or ATSs---is invisible to this analysis.

\textbf{Event-reaction null result.}
The null event-reaction finding (zero labeled executions within 30 seconds
of three macro releases) cannot be attributed with certainty to behavior
rather than data coverage.
Defense equities may simply be lower-priority trading venues for macro-event
algorithms, which typically focus on S\&P 500 futures and broad ETFs.

\textbf{Cluster interpretability.}
The strategy archetype labels assigned to each cluster (``stat\_arb'',
``momentum'', etc.) are heuristic and not validated against ground truth.
The silhouette score of 0.187 at $k=5$ indicates substantial within-cluster
heterogeneity.

\textbf{Short analysis window.}
25 trading days is insufficient to detect seasonal effects, earnings-cycle
patterns, or multi-week strategy evolution.

\subsection{What Remains for Final Submission}

The core pipeline is complete and all reported numbers are finalized.
Remaining work before final submission includes: expanding the literature
review with more recent empirical references (2022--2025), and adding
cross-firm comparison charts where STFL fill data are included alongside
UBSS.

\subsection{Extensions with Larger Data or Budget}

\textbf{Longer time series.}
A 12-month window would enable quarterly seasonality analysis and allow
detection of strategy changes around earnings.
The primary constraint is the LOBSTER data cost (\$500--\$2,000 per
ticker-year at academic pricing).

\textbf{Broader security universe.}
Extending to S\&P 500 constituents would provide enough fills across enough
firms to run cross-sectional PnL attribution and test whether the UBSS
short-horizon positive / EOD-negative pattern is idiosyncratic or systematic.

\textbf{Event-reaction with futures data.}
CME Globex MDP 3.0 data for ES and NQ futures would provide a cleaner test of
HFT event-reaction speeds, since futures react to macro events faster than
equities and MPID equivalents (Tag 1057 ``AggressorSide'') offer firm-level
attribution.

\textbf{Intraday strategy dynamics.}
With more fills, one could estimate \textit{intraday regime changes} ---
whether a firm shifts from market-making to directional trading around the open
or close, which is consistent with the UBSS opening-auction concentration
observed in our data.

\textbf{Adversarial ML on strategy classification.}
A natural extension is to test whether the classifier is robust to strategic
obfuscation: if a firm randomized its hidden/visible ratio, round-lot usage,
and directional persistence, could it evade classification?
This has direct relevance to regulatory surveillance.

\textbf{API cost considerations.}
The Step 3 firm-identification queries (MPID-to-firm-name resolution) consumed
approximately \$3--\$5 in Claude API calls (reflected in \texttt{api\_spend.csv}).
With a larger budget, the interpretation step---currently a one-time Claude
query per firm---could be expanded to generate per-firm strategic memos
comparing observed patterns against the full academic HFT taxonomy.

\section{Conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This project demonstrates that the LOBSTER MPID-labeled message stream contains
enough signal to characterize, attribute, and partially classify the behavior
of specific market participants from their external footprint alone.

The dominant empirical finding is one of \textbf{extreme order-flow sparsity
in fills}: the three largest firms by order count (WBPX, JPMS, WCHV) account
for 96\% of labeled volume while recording zero executions, consistent with
systematic passive quoting or data artifacts.
The sole substantially active firm in our sample (UBSS, 151 fills) exhibits
a U-shaped PnL profile under the corrected benchmark: positive at all measured
horizons (peaking at \$49/fill at 30 minutes), with a transient trough around
30 seconds.
This is more consistent with a directional position that experiences short-term
mean-reversion noise before the longer-horizon signal is confirmed, rather than
pure adverse selection.

The feature-engineering and classification pipeline shows that LOB execution
events are highly predictable by structural order characteristics (direction,
hidden status, round-lot flag), achieving 98.6\% accuracy.
The dominant regression finding --- short-horizon momentum strongly predicts
order direction with a mean-reversion sign --- is consistent with the
market-making hypothesis and robust to feature omission.

The event-reaction analysis (RQ2) yields a null result: no MPID-labeled
executions are detectable within 30 seconds of three macro releases on
these defense equities.
This is the most natural negative result in the project: defense-sector names
are unlikely to be the primary trading venue for macro event-driven HFT,
which concentrates on broad-market instruments.

The role of agentic AI tools was substantial throughout:
they compressed approximately 4 weeks of pipeline development into 4 days,
generated the full visualization suite, and assisted with literature synthesis
and anomaly interpretation.
The primary limitations were in data acquisition (where manual provisioning
remains irreplaceable) and in validating cluster interpretations (where
domain expertise is required to distinguish plausible from confirmed labels).
